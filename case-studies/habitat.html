Did Repair Investments Move the Needle? A Causal Study of Housing Program Outcomes

Testing whether visible improvements translate to measurable home-value gains

A community housing nonprofit spent $12.25M over fifteen years renovating homes in Philadelphia. We tried three independent methods to answer whether those renovations raised property values. None found a clear effect. The interesting work was in showing why — and what that tells us about the measurement instrument, not just the program.

The question we were asked

A community housing nonprofit funds renovations on owner-occupied homes in lower-income neighborhoods of West and North Philadelphia. The theory of change: home equity is a major component of generational wealth; renovations improve home value; therefore renovations are a wealth-building intervention.

We were asked to evaluate whether that theory was empirically supported — specifically, whether renovated homes appreciated faster than they would have without the renovation. The answer matters for donor reporting, funding conversations, and program design.

Three sources, each with different strengths and weaknesses: program repair records (address-level, with renovation date and cost); Zillow Zestimate (home-level annual estimates, 2016–2025); and the Zillow Home Value Index (ZIP-level, 2000–2025, longer history but coarser geography).

A structural feature shaped everything: renovations are concentrated. Two ZIP codes alone account for nearly half of all renovations; the top 10 ZIPs absorbed more than 80% of the funding. This is sensible program targeting, but it complicates causal identification — treatment is correlated with neighborhood characteristics that independently drive home values.

The identification challenge

The organization doesn't randomly assign which homes get renovated — they target homes that need help, in neighborhoods where need is concentrated. A simple "renovated vs. unrenovated" comparison conflates the renovation effect with selection effects. All three methods below are attempts to handle that confounding without a randomized experiment. Triangulating across them lets us make a stronger claim than any single method would support.

Approach 1 — Difference-in-differences

DiD compares how outcomes change between treated and untreated units, before and after treatment. We ran it twice: at the ZIP level (grouping ZIPs by total renovation spending) and at the home level (grouping homes by renovation year).

What we saw

  • At the ZIP level: ZIPs with the highest renovation investment had the lowest baseline home values — a direct artifact of targeting. Home values rose across all spending categories, including ZIPs with zero renovation, suggesting market trends dominated any program effect.
  • At the home level (122 homes, renovations 2016–2024): pre-renovation trends looked similar across cohorts. Post-renovation, no clear jump. Renovated homes rose with the market, not above it.
  • Event-study estimates produced wide 90% confidence intervals consistently crossing zero. We couldn't rule out a small effect; we couldn't claim one.

Approach 2 — Neighbor comparison

For each of 120 renovated homes, we compared its value trajectory to its actual neighbors who did not receive renovations. Two homes on the same block share most location-driven value movement, so post-renovation divergence should isolate the repair effect.

Pre-renovation, treated and neighbor trajectories tracked closely. Post-renovation, they continued to track closely. Treatment effects had wide confidence bands centered near zero across all post-period years. No clear effect.

Approach 3 — Synthetic control

Where DiD requires finding one comparison group, synthetic control constructs one: for each treated ZIP, a weighted combination of untreated ZIPs that closely matches the treated ZIP's pre-period trajectory. The post-period gap between the actual ZIP and its synthetic counterpart estimates the effect.

We applied this to 33 treated ZIPs with a donor pool of 13 untreated Philadelphia ZIPs. For a representative example (ZIP 19129), treated and synthetic trajectories tracked so closely post-renovation that the gap was indistinguishable from noise.

The placebo test strengthened this finding: re-running the procedure on never-renovated ZIPs, several untreated ZIPs showed larger "effects" than treated ones. If renovation truly moved values, treated ZIPs should be outliers on this measure. They weren't.

Putting the null in dollar terms

The point estimate from our preferred specification: renovations were associated with a $1,670 decrease in home value on average — but the 90% confidence interval crosses zero, so the honest read is "no detectable effect."

Why the null result is a measurement finding, not just a program finding

We did not find evidence that renovations raised home values. That is not the same as finding evidence that they didn't. The data can rule out large positive effects; it cannot rule out small ones. More importantly: Zillow's Zestimate is a market-trend estimator. It picks up neighborhood comparables. It is structurally unlikely to pick up interior repairs, mechanical systems, accessibility improvements, or any non-visible work — which is exactly what this program does.

The right read: using home value as a proxy for wealth, with Zillow as the measurement instrument, we don't see the program effect. That tells us as much about the instrument as it does about the program.

Recommendations

  • Expand outcome metrics beyond home value. Resident-reported safety, satisfaction, health, and housing stability are likely where program effects actually show up — but require survey instrumentation the organization doesn't currently have.
  • Improve the measurement instrument. Independent appraisals with pre/post pairs would capture interior improvements that Zestimate misses.
  • Record repair type in program data. Tracking what was repaired (roof vs. plumbing vs. accessibility) would enable subgroup analyses — average effects may mask heterogeneous ones.
  • Revisit the wealth-creation framing. If the goal is wealth-building, home equity may not be the right channel for this type of work. If the goal is housing stability and quality of life, the program likely works — but needs different instruments to demonstrate it.

What this project taught me

A null result, communicated carefully, is more useful than a positive result communicated carelessly. The discipline is to report a number alongside the range of values consistent with it — and to name the assumptions that had to hold for the number to be meaningful. "We estimate X but only under these conditions" serves stakeholders better than a single headline that loses its caveats the moment it's quoted.

Identification is mostly a data-collection problem. The methods — DiD, synthetic control — are well-documented. The hard part is longitudinal data, a clean donor pool, a pre-period long enough to test parallel trends, and an outcome variable that actually captures what you care about. Most of the effort here went into data assembly and outcome validity, not modeling.
