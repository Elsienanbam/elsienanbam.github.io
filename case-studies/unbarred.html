Unbarred: a RAG system that answers questions about local law without giving legal advice

Most Americans can't find the laws that govern their daily lives. We built a retrieval-augmented question-answering system over California county ordinances — and spent most of our time on the unglamorous problem of knowing whether retrieval was actually working.

The problem

County and municipal codes — the laws that govern housing, zoning, permits, noise, protest — sit in non-searchable PDFs scattered across thousands of separate government websites. Commercial databases like Westlaw exist, but they're priced for lawyers, not the small-business owner trying to read a home-occupancy ordinance.

Asking a general-purpose LLM produces confident, jurisdiction-agnostic answers that may or may not reflect the actual statute. Unbarred is the alternative: a retrieval-augmented system that answers in plain English but grounds every response in the actual local statute text, with citations users can verify.

The thing I'd most want a hiring manager to take from this project: the system itself is a few hundred lines of well-understood machinery. The interesting work was deciding how to evaluate whether retrieval was working, and building the discipline of measurement before iteration.

The pipeline

The system follows a standard RAG structure with a few important design decisions at each stage:

  • Ingest: county ordinance PDFs parsed and chunked along section boundaries (300–500 tokens), preserving hierarchical metadata (Title, Chapter, Section, county).
  • Index: FAISS during experimentation; Supabase pgvector in production.
  • Retrieve: semantic similarity search, filtered to the user's county before ranking, then reranked by cosine threshold.
  • Generate: Claude 3 Opus with a citation-mandatory prompt constrained to retrieved text only.
  • UI: chat surface with per-county filter, source citations, CSV export, and a persistent disclaimer that the tool is not legal advice.

Parsing: when the documents fight you

County codes are not written for machines. Headers shift mid-PDF, section numbering changes conventions, footers interrupt content. Across counties it gets worse — some use Title → Chapter → Section; others insert Articles, Divisions, or Parts at irregular depths.

We needed chunking that preserved legal hierarchy as metadata, not undifferentiated text. A user asking about "§ 1.12.010" needs to retrieve that section; the answer needs to cite "Title 1, Chapter 12, Section 010" — not a nearby paragraph that happens to mention it.

Approach: for each county, a LLaMA-based LLM inspected a sample of pages and proposed regex patterns for that county's headers and footers. We validated and tuned those patterns manually before running at scale. Output: a structured CSV per county where each row is one legal section with hierarchical metadata columns and full text at 300–500 tokens.

LLM-assisted pattern generation, validated manually, beats either pure manual parsing or pure LLM extraction. The LLM proposes structure well; humans catch when it's wrong.

Retrieval: comparing six approaches

We tested six retrieval configurations end-to-end against a held-out evaluation set:

  • BM25 — lexical retrieval; baseline.
  • FAISS with all-MiniLM-L6-v2 (384 dimensions).
  • FAISS with sentence-transformers/mpnet-base-v2.
  • FAISS with BAAI/bge-small-en-v1.5.
  • FAISS with a hybrid reranker (MPNet embeddings + RoBERTa-style rerank).
  • ColBERT — abandoned; failed to retrieve usable results in our setup.

We tracked two families of metrics:

  • Cosine-similarity metrics (Precision@10, Recall@10, MRR, nDCG@10) — measures whether retrieved chunks are semantically close to the ground-truth answer.
  • Section-match metrics (Section_Precision@10, Section_Recall@10, Section_Hit@3, Section_Exact_Match) — measures whether retrieved chunks come from the right legal section. For a legal QA system, this is what actually matters: semantic closeness doesn't save you if you're citing the wrong statute.

What the evaluation showed

The bge-small model scored highest on Section_Hit@3 and Section_Recall@10 — the metrics that matter most for legal precision. However, it was operationally incompatible with the production Supabase pgvector setup at the time. We made an explicit trade-off: ship all-MiniLM-L6-v2 in production, accept a modest drop in retrieval accuracy, gain operational simplicity and lower cost.

The largest precision win didn't come from the model. It came from filtering candidates by jurisdiction before embedding-based ranking. In a corpus where the same words mean different things in different counties, that single architectural decision did more for retrieval quality than any model swap.

Generation: model choice and constraints

We compared Claude 3 Opus and GPT-4 via a blind human evaluation: reviewers chose the more accurate, helpful, and readable response without knowing which model produced it. Claude won on majority preference, with reviewers noting fewer hallucinations and clearer citation structure.

The generation prompt has three jobs:

  • Constrain answers to retrieved chunks only. No training data, no speculation, no interpretive language.
  • Handle out-of-scope queries cleanly — no jurisdiction set, requests for legal outcomes, questions requiring subjective judgment.
  • Enforce a consistent output format — factual summary, then mandatory citations to the specific Title, Chapter, and Section.

The phrase that mattered most across many prompt iterations: "do not interpret, do not advise; summarize and cite."

What I'd do differently

  • Hybrid retrieval. Adding BM25 in parallel with the dense retriever would catch exact statute-number queries (e.g. "§ 1.12.010") that dense embeddings sometimes miss.
  • Cross-encoder reranking. A cross-encoder pass over the top-20 candidates would likely improve Section_Hit@3 further at modest latency cost.
  • Structured query parsing. A small LLM call to extract jurisdiction and question type from the raw query — before retrieval — would make the county filter more reliable than a UI dropdown users have to remember to set.
  • Coverage. The prototype handles a handful of California counties. Scaling to the long tail of US county formats is mostly an engineering and curation problem, but it gates any real-world impact.

What this project gave me

Retrieval quality is mostly a data problem, not a model problem. The headline-grabbing parts of an LLM system — the model, the prompts — mattered less than parsing PDFs without losing structure, building a held-out evaluation set with section-level ground truth, and picking the right metadata schema for filtering.

Evaluation discipline transfers. The instincts that make a good A/B test or causal study — define your outcome precisely, build a clean comparison, measure what matters not what's easy — apply directly to RAG evaluation. The vocabulary is different; the discipline is the same.

Public-interest ML has its own constraints. The hardest call wasn't technical: it was where to draw the line between making legal information accessible and inadvertently giving legal advice. We accepted that aggressive constraints — disclaimers, citation requirements, refusal to interpret — made the system less satisfying to use. In civic technology, precision is not just a technical concern.
