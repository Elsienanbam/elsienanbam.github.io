Evaluating a B2B Lead Scoring Model: Quality vs. Quantity in Practice

Using retrospective analysis to assess the volume-quality trade-off

A five-year-old B2B lead scoring model was producing high MQL volume and low conversion. The sales team suspected the model was qualifying too many low-quality leads. The question: if we change it, what happens — and how do we know before we break it?

Role
Data Analyst Intern, GTM Analytics
Duration
Summer 2024
Stack
SQL · Python · pandas · Tableau
Context
B2B fintech internship — methodology abstracted
Business question
A five-year-old lead scoring model was generating high MQL volume with low downstream conversion. Would raising the bar help — and by how much would it hurt pipeline?
My role
Sole analyst. Reconstructed the model from secondary data, built and ran the retrospective simulation, and presented the trade-off to GTM stakeholders.
Methods
Model reconstruction from multi-touch attribution data; retrospective simulation over 90-day lead cohorts; per-region breakdown of pipeline impact.
Key takeaway
Dropping MQL volume preserved >99.5% of marketing-generated pipeline value — reframing "volume loss" as load reduction with minimal revenue exposure.

The problem

A point-based lead scoring model, built years earlier over behavioral and demographic signals, was generating MQLs at a Marketing-Qualified-to-Sales-Qualified conversion rate consistently below the industry benchmark. The hypothesis: the model was awarding points too generously, so the bar for becoming an MQL was too low, and sales reps were drowning in leads they had no way to prioritize.

The GTM team wanted specific scoring changes implemented. My job was to determine whether those changes would help — before applying them globally and discovering the answer the expensive way.

The shape of the question matters. "Will this raise the conversion rate?" is the wrong question — raising the bar always raises the rate among those who clear it. The right question is the trade-off: does the gain in conversion rate exceed the loss in absolute pipeline value, and where in the distribution does the loss come from?

The data problem hiding inside the modeling problem

The first surprise: there was no source-of-truth dataset for the lead scoring model itself. The marketing automation platform only retained activity-level data for a fixed window, and historical exports were inconsistent. There was no table that said "lead X had score Y on date Z because of touchpoint W."

So before I could analyze the model, I had to reconstruct it:

  • Pull every touchpoint from the multi-touch attribution data.
  • Pull each lead's demographic profile (segment, region, role) from contact tables.
  • Apply the documented scoring rules to recreate, for each MQL, the chain of touchpoints that got them there.
  • Validate the reconstruction by checking that reconstructed scores matched model-assigned scores within tolerance.

The reconstruction was a close approximation — there were rules I couldn't fully replicate (score recycling, time-based decay, edge cases in demographic mapping). That limitation was named explicitly upfront, and it shaped the claims I could and couldn't make.

Lead scoring is a measurement problem before it's a modeling problem. If you can't reliably observe what your model did and why, you can't improve it — you can only swap it for a different model you also can't observe. The first deliverable wasn't a recommendation; it was a reproducible measurement of how the current model was behaving.

Diagnosing the current state

Three diagnostics defined the problem clearly:

1. Where the leads end up

For every MQL routed to sales, I tracked the engagement-status outcome — Attempting, Open, Rejected, Contacted, Meeting Set, Opportunity Created. The vast majority sat in states indicating no engagement or active rejection. A small minority showed any real interest signal. This was the strongest evidence that the model was qualifying volume, not quality.

2. Why the leads get rejected

Among rejected leads, two rejection reasons dominated: unresponsive (which could indicate a bad lead or a bad outreach pattern — a confound worth flagging) and not a fit (existing customer, product unavailable). The second category is the model's direct failure: those leads shouldn't have crossed the MQL threshold.

3. Which touchpoints drive low-quality MQLs

For each touchpoint type — webinars, first-party events, third-party events, content downloads — I measured: (a) what fraction of MQLs included that touchpoint, and (b) what fraction of those MQLs converted downstream. Some touchpoint types produced high MQL volumes with disproportionately low downstream conversion. Those were the model's leakiest gates.

The retrospective simulation

The proposed changes: differentiate point values for touchpoint sub-types the current model treated identically, reduce scores for the leakiest touchpoints, and align regional inconsistencies that had accumulated through years of one-off tweaks.

To estimate the impact without rolling out the changes, I ran a retrospective simulation:

  • For each lead that reached MQL status, walk back through their touchpoints in the 90 days prior.
  • Recompute the score they would have received under the proposed rules.
  • Check whether the recomputed score would still have crossed the MQL threshold.
  • For leads that still qualify, propagate their actual downstream outcomes — pipeline value, SQO conversion.
  • Aggregate: total MQL volume, SQO conversion, and pipeline value under both models on the same cohort.
Retrospective simulation bounds the structural impact of a model change. It cannot capture the behavioral impact — whether sales reps will convert more efficiently on a smaller, higher-quality lead set. That requires a pilot. Saying this upfront prevents the pilot recommendation from looking like an afterthought.

The headline trade-off

The simulation produced a result that looks alarming in isolation: under the proposed model, MQL volume drops substantially, SQO count drops, and pipeline value drops. If you stopped there, the proposal looks bad.

But the right comparison:

ComparisonDirection
MQL volume changeSubstantial decrease
30-day SQO count changeDecrease, smaller than MQL decrease
Pipeline value changeDecrease, smaller still
Pipeline value as fraction of total marketing-generated pipeline<0.5% — a rounding error

The model change drops volume disproportionately at the low-value end of the distribution. High-value opportunities are mostly preserved. The "loss" against total pipeline is tiny; the reduction in sales-rep load is large.

A per-region breakdown revealed substantial heterogeneity: regions whose lead mix skewed toward touchpoint types that lost points were affected far more than regions with a different mix. The same model change is not the same intervention everywhere.

Recommendations

  • Don't roll out globally based on the simulation alone. It establishes that the structural pipeline impact is small. It doesn't establish that sales reps will convert better on the new lead mix. Test that in a pilot first.
  • Run a single-region, quarter-long pilot. Pick the region with historically stable lead volume, sufficient downstream conversion to detect effects, and a mix that exposes the differentiation logic. Compare against that region's prior baseline and against unmodified regions as a DiD-style read.
  • Define success as pipeline value per unit of sales-rep effort — not conversion rate, which is mechanically guaranteed to rise when you raise the bar. Lead-engagement-status mix, time-to-disposition, and pipeline-value-per-rep are the more honest indicators.
  • Fix the data infrastructure. The biggest constraint on this analysis was the absence of a reliable scoring-activity table. Building a proper export pipeline from the marketing automation platform — capturing every score change, touchpoint, and threshold crossing — would make every future analysis ten times faster.

What this project taught me

Measurement is half the answer; framing is the other half. Two things made this work matter: a defensible methodology for estimating the impact of a change before making it, and a way of presenting the trade-off that didn't reduce a multi-dimensional decision to a single misleading number.

The model isn't the deliverable. The team was framing the problem as "fix the scoring model." What they actually needed was a better diagnostic loop — a way to tell when the model was drifting from sales-rep needs, and a way to test changes before committing to them. The scoring tweak was one output of that loop; building the loop itself was the bigger contribution.
